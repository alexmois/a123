What most would've anticipated as a battle of division leaders is instead a lopsided affair on Wednesday, at least on paper. The St. Louis Cardinals (59-78; before Tuesday's results) and Atlanta Braves (90-46) are crossing paths again. In April, the Braves went to St. Louis and brought out the brooms. Now, the Cardinals can leave their mark in Truist Park, like they did in the 2019 postseason. The first pitch for game two is scheduled to start at 7:20 p.m. EDT. Let's see what each team has in store.

 

*Published before Tuesday's game

A Sad St. Louis Season

They may be fresh off a win coming into this series, but there hasn't been much to celebrate in St. Louis this season. A rough year on the mound is the main reason why the Cardinals are last in the NL Central. That brings Dakota Hudson to the mound on Wednesday, getting the start for St. Louis. In 18.2 road innings, Hudson has 18 hits, 11 walks, four homers, and 11 runs (10 earned) charged to his name. Both of his starts as a visitor ended with the opponent scoring at least three runs against him. St. Louis' bullpen began the series 22nd in ERA, so that's not a group that should be trusted. Regardless of role, every Cardinals arm needs to bring their best against the Braves.

St. Louis' offensive has been moderately productive, putting up 4.49 runs per game (17th) this season. It's no surprise considering that Paul Goldschmidt and Nolan Arenado survived the trade deadline fire sale. The Cardinals are a top-10 team in hard-hit rate, home runs, walk rate, OBP, and slugging percentage. They're also 12th in strikeout rate. Baserunning gets St. Louis in trouble, but otherwise, the lineup is capable of doing damage.

Chop to the Top

Atlanta didn't disappoint this season, beating every team to 90 wins. Comfortably owning MLB's best record after taking three of four from the Dodgers, the rest of the Braves season will be about staying healthy. The rotation has dealt with the team's biggest blow this season, but Spencer Strider has been on the mound every fifth day. Strider is MLB's preeminent strikeout artist, leading qualified pitchers in K/9 and strikeout rate by a healthy margin. Nobody has more total punchouts. As for results, his last four starts have ended with the Braves celebrating another win. Strider had a 2.97 ERA in six August outings. Atlanta's relievers are strong too, ranking third in ERA and fifth in WAR, so don't sleep on the arms delivering the ball. 

Most people tend to ignore their pitching because the offense is MLB's best. Atlanta leads the majors in runs per game, batting average, OBP, slugging percentage, hard-hit rate, and home runs. They're also a top-10 team in strikeout rate, stolen bases, and stolen base percentage. Ronald Acuna Jr. is the NL MVP favorite, and Matt Olson is tied for the MLB lead in long balls. Of their nine regular hitters, the lowest OPS before this series began belonged to Eddie Rosario, and it was .784. The league average OPS is .735 this season. The Braves are lethal at the plate.

Full-Game Side Bet

Rating:

This one shouldn't be close. The Cardinals got swept by these Braves once already, at home where they're better, and all three losses were by at least three runs. St. Louis has found ways to lose this season, even when they were competing for most of it. Atlanta has dominated nearly everyone in their path.

Spencer Strider's run prevention is beginning to catch up with his elite swing-and-miss stuff if a dominant August (2.97 ERA, 46 strikeouts in 36.1 innings, one homer allowed, and a 5-1 Braves record) is any indication. At the plate, the Braves score against anyone, and the Cardinals are a bottom-10 team in run prevention this season. Strider will deliver again, the Braves will bash a mediocre Dakota Hudson, and Atlanta will cruise to a win at home.

Full-Game Total Pick

Insiders Status:

Rating:

Atlanta came into this series putting up 5.8 runs per game, the most in the majors. St. Louis came in at a modest 4.49 per contest. With Spencer Strider's 3.79 home ERA facing Dakota Hudson's 5.40 road ERA, this game will probably finish with double-digit runs.

These are two teams that hit the ball harder than almost everyone. Atlanta has MLB's highest hard-hit rate, while the Cardinals are third by that metric. Each club is top-10 in slugging homers too, which Truist Park tends to help hitters with. A hot Atlanta day, a pair of hittable pitchers, and two offenses that crush the ball should combine for a lot of runs.
