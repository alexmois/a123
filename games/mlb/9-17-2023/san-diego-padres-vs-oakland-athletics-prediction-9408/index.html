October won't be in the cards for the Oakland Athletics (46-101) this season, as the owners of MLB's worst record after Friday night. The San Diego Padres (70-78) are still alive, but their chances aren't looking great either. These teams will march closer to their inevitable fate on Sunday when they play each other for the final time. The first pitch is scheduled for 4:07 p.m. EDT in the Oakland Coliseum. Round one went to the Padres. Let's see if they'll close the series with another victory.

 

*Published before Saturday's game

Padres Blocking the Noise

As with most disappointing seasons, rumors begin swirling about a team's various issues. San Diego is dealing with another wave right now, but it didn't stop them from winning a series against the Dodgers this week. Nor did it hinder Friday's 8-3 win to begin this series. San Diego has been a mediocre offense for most of the year but is putting up 6.00 runs per game this month. Their run-scoring formula begins with MLB's best walk rate. The Padres are also top-10 in stolen bases (8th) and strikeout rate (8th). They have 192 home runs after Friday night as well. A .241 team batting average (22nd) has hindered San Diego's success, but this lineup is still dangerous when everything is clicking.

 

Pitching has been the Padres' strength all year, as only five teams are allowing fewer runs per game. The bullpen is mediocre (and part of the reason they struggle in close games), but the rotation has been strong. Rookie Pedro Avila has been getting starts for the club lately, to mixed results. Three of his five starts of the year have been San Diego victories. However, he has a 6.10 ERA as a starting pitcher in 20.1 innings, without mentioning five more unearned runs. As a reliever, he only allowed two total runs across 19.0 innings (0.95 ERA). He seems better suited for a relief role, albeit in a limited sample size. At least for one more appearance, he'll be a starter, hoping to avoid allowing at least five runs for the fourth time in a five-start stretch.

Oakland Back Below Everyone

Friday's loss put Oakland back in MLB's cellar, continuing their dance with the Royals at the bottom. However, all hasn't been awful lately. After spending a large chunk of the season on pace to be MLB's worst pitching staff ever, the Athletics aren't even the club allowing the most runs per game this season anymore. Sneaking their way up to 29th, Ken Waldichuk will try to keep it that way, at the very least. The southpaw has been better at home, with a 3.97 ERA in 56.2 innings. He's tossed 12.0 consecutive shutout innings (against playoff contenders), including six no-hit innings against the Astros last Monday. Overall, four of his last five starts have come against AL playoff hopefuls, and he has a 1.69 ERA in that span. Three of his last four outings have been Oakland wins. The Athletics have a strong case for MLB's worst bullpen, but Waldichuk can put the team in a strong position to succeed. 

That's if the offense, which is scoring the fewest runs per game, can dent the scoreboard. Listing what Oakland does well offensively starts and stops at stealing bases. The A's are fifth in stolen bases and base-stealing percentage. Otherwise, this team is last in batting average and slugging percentage. They're 29th (out of 30 teams) in hard-hit rate and OBP. Other statistics that Oakland is in the bottom third of league-wide include home runs, walk rate, and strikeout rate. Brent Rooker, Esteury Ruiz, and Shea Langeliers are exciting to watch. The rest of it is usually a chore.
