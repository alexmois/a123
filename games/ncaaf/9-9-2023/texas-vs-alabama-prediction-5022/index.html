The Texas Longhorns and Alabama Crimson Tide will square off on Saturday at Bryant-Denny Stadium. Kickoff from Tuscaloosa, AL, is at 7:00 p.m. ET.

Texas beat Rice 37-10 in its season opener. It has an over/under win total of 9.5 wins in 2023. The Longhorns were picked to win the conference title in the Big 12 preseason poll. They were 8-5 overall last season, including 2-2 in road games.

Alabama took care of business against Middle Tennessee in week one. It has an over/under win total of 10.5 wins this year. The Crimson Tide were predicted to finish second to Georgia in the SEC media preseason poll. They were 11-2 overall, including 7-0 at home last season.

'Bama is a seven-point spread favorite and the game total is 54.5 points.

Is Texas back? We'll find out soon enough

Texas beat in-state foe Rice at home in its only tune-up before a high-stakes battle with Alabama. The matchup presents an interesting juxtaposition. There is a growing belief that Texas is back, as former Nick Saban assistant Steve Sarkisian has recruited a wealth of talent to Austin since taking over as head coach in 2021. On the other side is the Crimson Tide, a team that's fighting to stay among the sport's elite programs following consecutive seasons without a national title. While Saturday's game won't close the book on either team's championship aspirations, the victor will earn the type of high-profile win needed to crack the four-team CFP field.

Sophomore quarterback Quinn Ewers paced the Horns in week one, completing 19 of his 30 passing attempts for 260 yards and four total scores. The Texas ground game was on point too, as sophomore Jaydon Blue (10 carries for 55 yards), sophomore Jonathon Brooks (12 carries for 52 yards), and freshman CJ Baxter (five carries for 38 yards) consistently moved the ball.

All-American candidate Xavier Worthy led the receivers with seven receptions for 90 yards, and five other pass-catchers hauled in at least two passes. As long as Ewers stays healthy, the passing offense could be electric, with Jordan Whittington (652 receiving yards), Georgia transfer Adonai Mitchell (three catches for 26 yards and a TD vs. Rice), Wyoming transfer Isaiah Neyor (20 yards-per-catch and 12 TDs), and tight end Ja’Tavion Sanders (613 receiving yards and five TDs) catching balls.

The Texas defense returns plenty of experience from a 2022 unit that finished second in the Big 12 in total and scoring defense. The run defense was dominant in week one, holding the Owls to 27 rushing yards on 25 attempts. The Horns also created three turnovers, forcing a fumble and picking off two passes.

The secondary is the backbone of the D, with Jerrin Thompson (53 tackles and seven passes defended), Jahdae Barron (42 tackles and two INTs), and Ryan Watts (three passes defended) in charge. Jaylan Ford (112 tackles) and true freshman Anthony Hill are two names to watch at linebacker and defensive tackles T’Vondre Sweat and Byron Murphy project as future NFL players.

Has Alabama taken a step back?

Alabama is used to being crowned the king of college football, but it has to get used to the fact that it has competition following Georgia's consecutive national title wins. Can it reclaim top-dog status in 2023?

The Crimson Tide manhandled the Blue Raiders last weekend, outscoring them 28-0 in the first half and 28-7 after halftime. Alabama rushed for 205 yards on 40 carries (5.1 YPC) and passed for 226 yards (70.8% completion percentage), as sophomore QB Jalen Milroe was the stat-leader in both categories (242 total yards and five total touchdowns). Middle Tennessee managed just three yards per carry against the Crimson Tide's stout front and 3.8 yards per completion against their lockdown secondary.

Saturday's game will be Alabama's first important game since it beat Kansas State by 25 in the Sugar Bowl, its 11th bowl/CFP victory in the last eight seasons. Simply put, these are the games the Tide win — and it's rarely competitive. Without QB Bryce Young, however, the likelihood of a blowout is low.

The number one pick in the 2023 NFL Draft was the most important player in college football a year ago, carrying the Crimson Tide on his back against the Longhorns — Sarkisian likened him to a "Houdini act" after last season's 20-19 Alabama victory in Austin — and in conference competition. If the offensive line is still questionable in protection, it will put too much pressure on the untested sophomore Milroe. Should it be too much, though, the Tide have capable reserves in ex-Notre Dame starter Tyler Buchner and ballyhooed frosh Ty Simpson.

Whoever is under center will take advantage of a more seasoned receiving unit in 2023. Leading pass-catchers Jermaine Burton (40 receptions for 677 yards and seven scores) and JaCorey Brooks (39 receptions for 674 yards and eight scores) return, and sophomore Isaiah Bond made his mark last week with five catches for 76 yards and a TD. Senior running backs Jase McClellan and Roydell Williams will handle the bulk of the carries early in the year until star frosh Justice Haynes is experienced.

The Crimson Tide defense ranked 12th in the nation in total defense, ninth in scoring defense, and No. 1 in the SEC in pass efficiency defense last season. It wasn't always stout against the run and didn't pick off enough passes, though. The defensive line is loaded with space eaters and pass rushers, with sophomore Jaheim Oatis and senior Tim Smith among the unit's most feared players.

The linebacking unit and secondary are littered with playmakers, too. Senior LB Chris Braswell (21 tackles and three sacks) will take over for Will Anderson at the JACK spot, junior LB Dallas Turner (21 tackles and four sacks) is on the other side, Deontae Lawson (51 tackles and four PBUs) will be the man in the middle, and Georgia transfer Trezmen Marshall will be another player to watch. CB Kool-Aid McKinstry (35 tackles and 15 PBUs) has first-round NFL talent, Louisiana transfer Trey Amos (36 tackles and eight PBUS) will be the other corner, and safeties Malachi Moore (31 tackles and five PBUs) and Jaylen Key (INT vs. MTSU) round out the defensive backfield.

The Longhorns may be relevant again but don't expect another close game between these teams in Tuscaloosa.

The Alabama defense is going to give Texas a rude awakening. Sure, the Longhorns have star talent, but wait 'til you see what the locked-in Bama defense does to them on Saturday. The Crimson Tide were almost robotic in their shutdown performance against Middle Tennessee and will look to expose Ewers and Texas as frauds for even entering the conversation of college football's elite.

On offense, Alabama will score enough to win and cover. It's a disciplined team (zero turnovers and two penalties in week one) ‚Äî but that's hardly surprising given the accountability on the Bama roster. This is a professional program brimming with players with realistic NFL aspirations. They understand if they're not accountable on every play, there's a guy behind them ready and able to take their job. The Tide committed 15 penalties against Texas last season, something Saban has likely reminded his players about more than once this week.

Milroe's dual-threat ability will be a factor on Saturday, forcing the Longhorns to take some of their attention off the Crimson Tide's other playmakers. That will prove deadly on several occasions, giving Bama the cushion it needs to comfortably cover the seven-point spread.

That will result in fewer points, as the Horns will need to work for every first down, hitting check-downs and throwing the ball away in favor of taking sacks. The Alabama defensive line is stout enough to hold up against the run, and the secondary will be itching to cause the type of turnovers that strike fear in the Longhorns' hearts. The Horns held Rice to 27 rushing yards, and the Tide do not have a bonafide workhorse running back they can rely on. Milroe is going to have to be careful with the ball when dropping back to pass and even more cautious when he's taking off to run with the football.
